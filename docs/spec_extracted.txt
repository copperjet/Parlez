1. Product Overview


1.1 The Problem
Millions of people spend years learning French in school — memorising grammar rules, conjugation tables, and vocabulary lists — and still cannot hold a basic conversation. The failure is not theirs. Classroom French is built around reading and writing. Speaking is a different skill, and it only develops through practice.
No app has solved this cleanly. Most pile on gamification, streak tracking, word drills, and badges. The learner is entertained but not speaking. Parlez removes everything except the one thing that works: real conversation.

1.2 The Solution
Parlez is a voice-first AI conversation app. The user opens the app and speaks French. The AI — a warm, patient French conversationalist named Marie — speaks back, listens, gently corrects mistakes, remembers what the user struggles with, and builds on every session. There is nothing else. No dashboard, no scores, no grammar lessons. Just the conversation.

Core Principle: The only goal of this app is to get the user speaking French.
Every design decision, feature, and AI behaviour must serve that goal. If it does not help the user speak, it does not ship.

1.3 Target User
Primary: Adults (18–45) who have some prior French education — school, university, or self-study — but have never reached spoken fluency. They know more French than they think they do. They are blocked by anxiety, lack of practice, and the absence of a judgment-free space to make mistakes.
Secondary: Absolute beginners willing to learn by doing, not by drilling.
The user's motivation is practical: travel, career, family, or a lifelong ambition to speak French. They are not hobbyists. They want to actually be able to speak, and they want to feel progress.

1.4 North Star Metric
Users who complete 10 sessions speak more French in session 10 than they did in session 1.
This is the only success metric that matters at launch.

3. User Flow


3.1 First-Time User Flow

Design principle: The user should be in their first conversation within 60 seconds of downloading the app.
Onboarding is the enemy of starting. Keep it ruthlessly short.

Step 1 — App Open (0–5 seconds)
Splash screen with the app name and tagline. No animation longer than 1.5 seconds. Immediately proceeds to onboarding.

Step 2 — Level Assessment (5–25 seconds)
A single screen with one question and four choices. No account creation required at this stage.

Screen title: "How much French do you know?"
Option A: "Nothing yet — I'm starting fresh"
Option B: "A little — I know some words and phrases"
Option C: "Some — I studied French before but can't really speak it"
Option D: "Decent — I can have basic conversations"
The selected level seeds Marie's initial conversation difficulty. It is not shown to the user again. No labels like 'beginner' or 'intermediate' are used.

Step 3 — Microphone Permission (25–35 seconds)
Standard OS permission prompt. Preceded by a single sentence explaining why: "Parlez listens to your voice so Marie can speak with you." If denied, the app shows a friendly prompt to enable permissions in Settings before proceeding.

Step 4 — Introduction (35–60 seconds)
Marie speaks first. Her opening is always warm, brief, and in French — calibrated to the chosen level. A gentle prompt appears on screen: "Respond when you're ready. Tap the mic or just speak."
The user is now in conversation. Onboarding is over.

3.2 Returning User Flow
Returning users go directly to the conversation screen. There is no home screen. Marie acknowledges that they have spoken before and picks up where the conversation left off thematically — or begins a fresh topic if the last session was more than 48 hours ago.
No login screen (account optional; sessions stored locally and synced when account exists)
Session history is used by the AI but not shown to the user
Load time from app open to Marie speaking: under 3 seconds

3.3 In-Session Flow
User opens the conversation screen
Marie speaks (audio plays, animated waveform shown)
Recording indicator activates. User speaks in French, English, or a mix
Speech-to-text transcribes in real time. Transcription shown on screen as faint text while recording
User finishes speaking (silence detection or manual tap)
AI processes response. Brief thinking animation displayed
Marie speaks her response. Text shown on screen simultaneously for reference
Repeat from step 3

3.4 Session End Flow
There is no formal session end. The user simply closes the app or navigates away. The AI does not ask "Are you done?" When the app reopens, Marie's first words acknowledge the gap since last time, naturally.


4. Conversation Interface


4.1 Screen Layout
The conversation screen is the entire app. There is no navigation bar, no menu, no sidebar. One screen, one purpose.

Zone
Content & Behaviour
Top bar
Marie's name and avatar (left). Settings icon (right). No other elements.
Conversation area
Scrollable. Shows current and recent exchanges as speech bubbles. Marie left, user right. Correction cards appear inline below Marie's bubble.
Waveform zone
Visual feedback showing Marie speaking (animated) or user speaking (live waveform). Quiet when neither is talking.
Mic button
Large central button. Tap to speak / tap to stop. Auto-activates after Marie finishes speaking (with brief 0.8s grace period).

4.2 Speech Bubbles
Marie's bubbles: left-aligned, soft background colour
User's bubbles: right-aligned, accent colour
Each bubble shows the spoken text as it was said (not corrected) — accuracy matters for the user seeing their own French
Correction cards appear as a small card directly below a Marie bubble, only when a correction is warranted
Bubbles older than the last 4 exchanges are scrolled out of view but accessible by scrolling up

4.3 Correction Cards
When Marie identifies a meaningful error, a correction card appears inline below her response. It is compact, non-alarming, and immediately clear.

Format: What you said → What to say
English gloss if the grammar point benefits from a brief explanation (one line maximum)
Corrections appear for at most 1–2 errors per turn to avoid overwhelming the user
Minor errors (accent marks, minor mispronunciation) are noted naturally in Marie's speech, not as cards
The card disappears from view as conversation scrolls but remains in scroll history

4.4 Input Modes
Primary: Voice. The mic button is always prominent.
Fallback: If the user long-presses the mic button, a text input field appears. This is for accessibility and noisy environments only — it is not promoted.
Language mixing is allowed: the user may speak in English or a mix. Marie always responds in French, with contextual support.

4.5 Settings Screen
Accessed via the settings icon in the top bar. This screen contains only:
Speech speed: slider from Slow / Normal / Fast (controls Marie's TTS playback speed)
Marie's voice: choice of 2–3 distinct French voices (all native-quality TTS)
Microphone sensitivity: Auto / Manual threshold
Haptic feedback: On / Off
Account: Sign in / Sign up (optional, enables cross-device sync)
Clear session history: Resets Marie's memory of the user (with confirmation)
Nothing else. No notification settings, no theme options, no language settings (French is the language; this is not configurable).

5. AI Conversation Engine


Marie is not a chatbot with French responses. Marie is a skilled language tutor whose method is natural conversation.
The distinction is critical: every response serves the user's speaking development, not just the conversational thread.

5.1 Persona
Attribute
Specification
Name
Marie
Character
Warm, patient, encouraging, genuinely curious about the user's life
Tone
Like a French friend helping you practice — not a teacher grading you
Language default
French-first, always. Code-switches to English only when a correction or explanation genuinely requires it
Correction style
Integrates corrections naturally into conversation before adding a correction card when needed
Patience
Never rushes the user. Comfortable with pauses and hesitation.
Encouragement
Genuine, specific, and never condescending

5.2 Language Level Adaptation
Marie adapts her vocabulary, sentence length, and grammatical complexity to the user's current ability in real time — not just based on the onboarding level.

Level A (beginner): Short sentences, common vocabulary, speaks slower, repeats key phrases, asks simple yes/no or choice questions
Level B (elementary/pre-intermediate): Longer sentences, introduces past tense, asks open questions, uses everyday idioms with brief explanations
Level C (intermediate): Natural speed, full range of tenses, cultural references, challenges user to elaborate and explain
Progression: Marie promotes the user's internal level when they consistently handle current-level responses without errors. This is invisible to the user.
Regression: Marie reduces complexity temporarily if the user is struggling, without comment. She never tells the user their level.

5.3 Correction Behaviour
The correction framework is central to the app's pedagogy. It is built around one principle: corrections must not interrupt the flow of conversation or make the user feel judged.

5.3.1 Correction Tiers
Tier
Type of Error
Marie's Response
Tier 1
Minor: accent, liaison, minor mispronunciation
Uses the correct form naturally in her reply. No card. No comment.
Tier 2
Meaningful: wrong tense, incorrect gender, word order
Incorporates the correct form into her reply ('Oui, tu es allé — et moi aussi...'). Generates a correction card.
Tier 3
Communication-breaking: meaning unclear or lost
Gently asks for clarification in French, offering scaffolding. Uses the opportunity to model the correct expression.

5.3.2 Correction Limits
Maximum 2 correction cards per AI turn
If the user makes 4+ errors in one turn, Marie addresses the 1–2 most important ones only
Consecutive turns: if the same error recurs, Marie addresses it once more clearly, then notes it in the learning profile
Marie never says 'That was wrong' or 'You made a mistake'. She models correct usage.

5.4 Memory and Learning Profile
The learning profile is the intelligence layer that makes Parlez more useful over time. It is maintained per-user, persists across sessions, and is invisible to the user.

5.4.1 What Is Tracked
Recurring grammatical errors (e.g., consistent confusion with passé composé vs imparfait)
Vocabulary gaps (words the user avoids or substitutes with English)
Topics the user engages with confidently vs. hesitantly
Pronunciation patterns (from STT confidence scores)
Speaking pace and length of turns over time
Overall level trajectory (rising, plateauing, or struggling)

5.4.2 How Memory Is Used
Marie revisits tracked difficulty areas naturally in conversation ('À propos, tu m'as parlé de ton travail — c'est quoi exactement?')
Marie does not say 'You often struggle with X'. She addresses it through practice.
At the start of a new session, Marie acknowledges the previous conversation and uses tracked profile to pitch her opening at the right level
Memory is stored locally on-device and optionally synced to a user account
Memory resets only on explicit user request (see Settings)

5.5 Conversation Topics
Marie drives the conversation with natural, engaging topics. She rotates through topic domains to give the user practice across different vocabulary areas. She always uses a topic that connects to something the user has mentioned before when possible.

Domain
Example Topics
Daily life
Morning routines, meals, commute, weekend plans
Personal
Family, friends, hometown, where they live
Work & study
Job, studies, ambitions, colleagues
Travel
Places visited, dream destinations, experiences abroad
Culture & tastes
Films, music, books, sport, food
French culture
Parisian life, French customs, regional differences, food culture
Opinions
Simple debates on familiar topics at higher levels

Marie always asks one question per turn. She does not ask two questions. If she has multiple things to say, she chooses the most relevant question and lets the conversation develop.

6.2 Text-to-Speech (TTS)
Marie's voice is the product. It must be natural, warm, and clearly French — not robotic, not generic, not generic-European.

6.3 Turn Management
Natural conversation has rhythm. The app must not feel like a push-to-talk radio. The following rules govern turn-taking:

Marie finishes speaking before the mic activates for the user
0.8-second grace pause after Marie finishes before mic auto-activates (prevents user rushing)
Visual mic-active indicator appears clearly when the user's turn begins
User can tap the mic at any time to interrupt Marie (Marie stops speaking)
If the user is silent for 4 seconds after the mic activates, Marie offers a gentle prompt in French ('Tu veux dire quelque chose?')
If silence continues for 8 more seconds, Marie continues the conversation with a simpler question

7.5 AI System Prompt Design
Marie's personality, behaviour, and correction logic are encoded in a structured system prompt sent with every API call. The system prompt includes:

Persona definition (warmth, patience, curiosity, French-first language policy)
Level instructions (derived from onboarding and current profile estimate)
Learning profile summary (recurring errors, flagged gaps, topics discussed)
Correction behaviour rules (tiers, maximum corrections per turn, natural integration)
Turn structure rules (one question per turn, do not lecture, do not explain grammar unless asked)
Session context (how long since last session, what was discussed last time)
Output format rules (main response, correction card data as structured JSON, note-to-self for profile update)

The AI response is structured JSON, not plain text.
Fields: speech_text (what Marie says), corrections[] (array of correction card objects), profile_notes[] (internal observations to store), level_signal (up/down/hold).
The app renders the speech_text as audio via TTS and displays corrections as cards. Profile notes are stored silently.

10.2 Error Handling
STT failure: display 'I didn’t catch that — try again?' on screen; Marie speaks it too
AI API failure: Marie says she is having a small problem and will be back in a moment; retry automatically after 3 seconds
TTS failure: show Marie’s text response on screen with a replay icon; do not block conversation
No internet: show offline banner; switch to on-device STT; AI responses served from a small local fallback model if possible, or show a message that full conversation requires internet
All error messages are in English for clarity; Marie’s in-conversation error messages are in French

12. Success Metrics


Parlez does not use engagement metrics as a success measure. Time spent in the app is not a goal. A user who has three 10-minute sessions a week and is visibly improving is a perfect user.
